The Scale-Up Investment Scheme (SUIS)

A growth-stage vehicle that begins where EIS legally stops, aimed at the £5m–£50m rounds where UK companies either scale or sell to an overseas buyer. The state does not give a tax rebate; it co-invests alongside private money, takes a capped equity stake, and recycles the proceeds. It is built to be the vetted pipeline the Mansion House pension money is waiting for, and to be bought in slices small enough for an ordinary saver.

The proposal. Hold the existing £0.95bn EIS, SEIS and VCT relief envelope and stand up a new growth tier delivered as government co-investment, not relief. The state matches private capital 25p in the pound, caps its own return, and gifts the surplus back to investors. Across a diversified book the winners repay the stake, so the Exchequer ends near break-even while the investor keeps a full return. It plugs the scale-up gap, supplies the UK assets the Mansion House Accord needs, and turns a single-company gamble into a downside-protected bet.

The downside floor (per £1, total loss)

On a complete loss, a 45%-rate retail investor keeps loss relief on the 75p at risk. The most they lose is about 41p in the pound. Pension money is already sheltered, so its floor comes from the co-investment structure rather than relief.

The Fiscal Case · Cost, Value & Mechanism · 2 of 5v0.4 · illustrative

What it costs, what it buys

A matched equity stake, not a rebate. The state co-invests beside private money, caps its own return, and hands the surplus back. Across a diversified book the winners repay the stake, so the Exchequer ends near break-even while more capital reaches companies than relief would deliver. Cost and value sit in three separate ledgers, kept apart here.

Bottom line. Figures here are per annual cohort — the unit the scheme runs on. One central cohort, both retail and pension matched at 25%: about £3.8bn of private capital mobilised, £4.7bn deployed into companies, a Treasury position of roughly break-even over the life of the book, ~£2.8bn of additional GVA, and ~£9bn of investor wealth at maturity. How the waterfall works

The investor commits £1. The government co-invests 25p alongside, so £1.25 reaches the company. Both hold equity in proportion. On exit, three rules apply in order:

1 · FloorThe taxable investor keeps loss relief on any shortfall, as under EIS. Downside protection is preserved.
2 · CapThe government takes back its 25p plus a modest fixed return (capital + ~3% a year), and no more.
3 · GiftAny value above that cap on the state's stake is handed to the investor, lifting their return.

This is the Enterprise Capital Fund waterfall: the state caps its own upside to pull private money in rather than crowd it out. Because winners repay the capped stake reliably across 20 or more deals, the government can afford to give the surplus away and still end roughly even. Selection sits with private fund managers; the state expresses no view on any single company.

Diversity is load-bearing. The state can only give the upside away because, across a diversified book, winners repay its stake reliably. On one deal that repayment is a coin toss and the structure breaks. This is why retail access must be pooled-fund only.

The ledger flips with equity

Exchequer net position per £1bn of private capital. Once the state holds equity rather than writing a cheque, the sign changes from cost to roughly break-even, and the investor is no worse off.

The hinge: additionality

Additionality is the share of investment that is genuinely new rather than displaced. Below about 39% the scheme destroys value; above it, it creates value. HMRC's EIS evaluations cannot pin it down precisely, so the policy's worth is an empirical bet on keeping it above 50%, protected by tight qualifying rules and the discipline of institutional co-investment.

Why co-invest, and why not just expand EIS

A rebate is money the state never sees again; a matched equity stake is an asset it is repaid from. That single change moves the Exchequer from a £393m cost to roughly break-even per £1bn, and a visible match also draws more take-up than a rebate of equal value (the H&R Block field trial found matching roughly doubled participation). EIS itself is the wrong vehicle to stretch: its size and age limits exclude scaling companies, so loosening it would inflate seed valuations rather than fund Series B and C. A separate co-investment tier aims at the gap the Treasury already recognises, costs less per pound, and gives the Mansion House pension money somewhere vetted to go.

What already exists, and what is missing

The UK has tried most of the pieces separately: blanket reliefs at the seed end, a co-investment bank for funds, and one-off equity vehicles in a crisis. None combines retail access, growth-stage targeting and a self-funding equity structure. That combination is the gap SUIS fills.

What SUIS borrows

From ECFThe capped-return waterfall: the state takes a limited return and leaves the upside to private investors, so it crowds capital in.
From BPCFund-of-funds delivery, so selection sits with private managers and the state never picks a company.
From Help-to-BuyMatched equity with a shared gain, and the visible match that drove take-up.
From EISRetail access and the loss-relief floor, kept so ordinary savers can take part safely.

What is new

Each existing instrument has one or two of the needed features. None has all four at once:

StageAimed squarely at the £5m–£50m scale-up gap, not seed (EIS/SEIS) or general VC (ECF/BPC).
AccessOpen to retail and pension money together, matched on the same terms. ECF and BPC are institutional only; the reliefs are retail only.
CostSelf-funding by design, unlike the reliefs, which are permanent gifts.
SelectionCrowd and manager-led. The state sets the stage, not the sector or the company.

On "picking winners"

EIS and SEIS do not pick individual companies, but they do pick categories: the qualifying-trade rules decide which sectors and company types are eligible, and HMRC polices that boundary closely. So the state already makes a structural bet about what deserves support. SUIS narrows that bet to a single dimension, company stage, and hands every company-level and sector-level choice to private fund managers risking their own investors' money. The state chooses where to stand; the crowd chooses what to back. That is a smaller claim to foresight than the present schemes make, not a larger one.

The Package · Stamp Duty & Affordability · 4 of 5v0.4 · illustrative

Abolish stamp duty on shares, then add SUIS

The two reforms attack the same problem from opposite ends. Abolishing stamp duty on shares lowers the cost of equity capital and lifts the secondary market; SUIS pushes primary capital into the growth-stage gap that abolition leaves untouched. Together they fit inside the £5bn envelope.

The case for abolition. The UK's 0.5% charge on share purchases is the highest equity-transaction tax of any major economy, and it still applies inside ISAs and SIPPs. It raises about £3.7bn a year but raises the cost of capital for UK firms and pushes investors toward overseas markets and exempt instruments. AIM shares have been exempt since 2014, so abolition mainly affects the main market, the venue large UK companies are leaving.

Why stamp duty is the strongest single cut

Bang per ££2.89 of long-run GDP per £1 of cost, the top of the efficiency table and far above income-tax cuts.
Self-fundingPlausibly recovers much of its cost through higher trading, higher prices and a lower cost of capital.
Hits saversIt is the one charge an ISA does not shelter you from. Every UK-share ISA holder pays it on purchase.
No country matchNo other major exchange levies a comparable tax. It is a self-imposed handicap on the FTSE.

The complementarity matters. Abolition makes UK equity cheaper to hold and trade, helping every holder including ISA savers. It does nothing, on its own, for the company that cannot raise a Series B at home. SUIS does exactly that. One fixes the secondary market; the other fixes primary growth finance.

Note on the SUIS link. Today an ISA does not exempt you from stamp duty, so raising the ISA allowance does nothing for the cost-of-capital drag on UK equity. Abolition does, which is why it is a better use of the same money than the ISA-allowance idea it replaces.

Three reasons the fit is better than it looks

CappableA co-investment fund has a set budget. Relief is demand-led and uncapped. Set the fund at £1bn a year and the high case cannot happen.
RecoverableThe £3.7bn for stamp duty is lost every year. The co-investment is a one-off deployment expected back with a return. Adding them as equals overstates the burden.
Off-deficitEquity purchases are financial transactions; like the Future Fund and the British Business Bank, they sit largely outside headline borrowing. The honest deficit cost of the SUIS leg is close to its expected loss, near zero, not the £0.94bn gross.

So on the crudest accounting the package lands near £4.6bn at central uptake. On realistic accounting, stamp duty is almost the whole cost, and the co-investment leg is mostly a balance-sheet movement expected to return.

The package

Abolish stamp duty on shares (~£3.7bn a year) and add a capped SUIS co-investment fund (~£1bn a year, expected to return). Combined headline cost about £4.7bn, inside the £5bn envelope, and falling sharply once recovery and financial-transaction accounting are applied. Neither is a saving; both are the highest-growth use of £5bn of fiscal room. The first lowers the cost of capital for every UK company; the second gets money into the ones that cannot raise it at home. They are complements, not rivals.

Synthesis & Conclusion · 5 of 5v0.4 · illustrative

If I had £5bn a year of tax-cutting room

Abolish stamp duty on shares, and run a Scale-Up Investment co-investment fund. The honest position depends on the horizon: within five years it is a real cost that buys near-term growth; in the long run it broadly pays for itself and leaves the economy permanently larger. Both views are shown below.

BLUF. With £5bn a year of room I would abolish stamp duty on shares and run a SUIS co-investment fund. Within five years the package is a genuine cost of about £18.5bn — almost entirely the stamp-duty revenue forgone — that buys roughly £6bn of near-term scale-up GVA and seeds a much larger long-run gain. In the long run it broadly self-funds: the ~£10.7bn a year of extra GDP from cheaper equity capital generates roughly enough tax to cover the cost, while the economy stays permanently larger and the fund recycles toward zero net call. It is not neutral at year five, and it is not meant to be: it is a near-term cost for a durable structural gain, with no new cliff edge and a mechanism that is repaid rather than gifted.

Why removing SDRT is the point

A tax cut pays for itself in the long run if its GDP multiplier beats 1 ÷ the tax-to-GDP ratio. At a 35% ratio that threshold is 2.86. The estimated multiplier for abolishing stamp duty on shares is 2.89 — just above it.

MechanismRemoving the 0.5% charge lowers the cost of equity capital, lifts investment and valuations, and raises GDP by about £10.7bn a year at maturity (2.89 × £3.7bn).
Self-fundingThat extra GDP generates roughly £3.7bn a year of tax at a 35% take — about equal to the £3.7bn cost. Net long-run fiscal cost approaches zero.
The prizeA permanently larger economy at roughly no standing fiscal cost. That is the whole case for abolition; without it, there would be no reason to give up the revenue.
Why it is not in Table 1. The GDP level effect is reached over roughly a decade, so little of it lands inside the five-year window. Counting SDRT's full cost in the short run but its benefit only in the long run is honest, not pessimistic — it is simply when each arrives.

One coherent argument

The thread runs straight. The objection to a tax-free overtime band was that it adds a cliff edge and rewards relabelling, not work. The disciplined alternative is to spend fiscal room removing distortions and filling genuine gaps. Stamp duty abolition removes the clearest distortion in the capital-market tax base and broadly pays for itself in growth; the co-investment fund fills the clearest gap in growth finance, repaid rather than gifted.

Sequence

Year 1Abolish SDRT. Launch the SUIS fund inside the British Business Bank, capped at £1bn a year. Both tiers live from the start: certified sophisticated/HNW angels direct, all other retail through the pooled fund.
Years 2–5A new cohort annually; designate SUIS firms toward the Mansion House 5% UK allocation; match retail and pension (pension at a lower rate if double-subsidy is challenged).
Year 8–12First SUIS cohorts mature (7-year hold) and recycle; SDRT growth dividend matures toward self-funding, so the annual call falls toward zero.

Conclusion

Work out the cliff edges we already have rather than inventing new ones. Within five years this package costs about £18.5bn — a real, mostly-stamp-duty cost — and buys around £22bn of near-term GVA plus the foundations of a durable gain. In the long run it broadly self-funds, because the £2.89-per-£1 growth dividend on stamp duty just clears the threshold at which a cut pays for itself, leaving the economy permanently larger while the fund recycles toward a zero net call. It is a near-term cost for a structural prize, it leaves the investor whole, and it adds no new distortion. It is the more disciplined use of the money, and it is the one I would make.

The punchline: once running at scale, the fund returns about £9.3bn a year of wealth to British savers — over a decade of mature operation roughly £93bn, about one entire HS2 (£102.7bn) — but at close to zero net public cost. The same scale as the railway, on the opposite side of the ledger: private wealth created, not public money spent.
